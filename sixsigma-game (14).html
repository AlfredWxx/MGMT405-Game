Scenario 1 of 4
AI Email Assistant (Copilot)
You recently started a new graduate role at a company that has just introduced Microsoft Copilot to help employees with writing and communication. Since you've already used AI tools a lot in school, you're excited that the company encourages employees to use it.

One afternoon, you're asked to respond to a client who has questions about a recent invoice and some changes to their service agreement. You use Copilot to draft the reply, and it produces a professional-sounding email that clearly explains the situation. Since you now have permission to communicate directly with clients, sending the response quickly would make a good impression.

However, you realize that the explanation in the email includes details about the billing changes that you haven't personally verified.
What is one risk of relying on the AI-generated email?
What should you do before sending it to the client?

Scenario 2 of 4
AI-Enabled Accounting Software
You are a controller at a mid-sized company that recently upgraded its accounting system to Sage Intacct, which now includes AI-assisted features such as automated financial forecasts, close automation, and consolidation across multiple entities.

At the end of the quarter, the system automatically generates a consolidated financial report and highlights several unusual trends in inventory costs and asset depreciation. The report looks complete and saves your team hours of manual work, and management is eager to use the numbers in their quarterly presentation.

However, because much of the process is now automated, your team did not manually review every output the system performed.
What is one risk of relying on the automated system in this situation?
How should a professional mitigate that risk before using the report?

Scenario 3 of 4
AI Audit Analytics (MindBridge)
Your public accounting firm recently adopted MindBridge, an AI-powered audit analytics tool that analyzes large volumes of financial transactions and highlights unusual patterns or potential risks. The software promotes its ability to provide oversight, explainability, and reliable insights, and many teams rely on it during audit planning.

While working on a new audit engagement, the system flags several transactions as low risk and indicates that no unusual activity was detected in certain areas. When you ask about it, the senior on the engagement says the tool is very reliable and suggests you can rely on its results when planning your audit procedures.

As a new auditor, you are still learning how the tool works and how the results are generated.
What is one risk of relying on the AI tool in this situation?
How should a professional respond before relying on its conclusions?
